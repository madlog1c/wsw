Life Span Mental Health Physical Health
Marine Mammal Science found the average lifespan of captive orcas to be only 12 years.
In the wild, orcas live 30-90+ years
Captive orcas suffer boredom, depression, psychosis, aggression, bullying from other captive orcas, isolation from family, and even total isolation from all other cetaceans (solitary confinement like Kiska).
Captive females are often artificially inseminated at extremely young ages, with little to no family support (all 5 of Kiska’s calves born in captivity have perished).
In the wild these are intelligent, social, family oriented animals, that live their lives together in pods, teaching each other lessons and culture from past generations and the oceans they live in.
All captive male orcas have a collapsed dorsal fin, which is very rare in the wild.
Captive orcas suffer extreme tooth decay and wear (often to the point of losing all their teeth) from grinding on their cages and tanks.
Tiny concrete pools drastically limit (or eliminate) their ability to dive and swim at high speeds.
Captive orcas get sun burns and cataracts from sun exposure due to shallow unnatural water.

Source: SeaWorld Fact Check / Whiting 2015

THE JOURNEY TO SAVE SMOOSHI

Phil Demers shares a unique bond with Smooshi the walrus. During his 12-year tenure at Marineland, Phil became Smooshi's surrogate mother, an event transcending the boundaries of species.

However, witnessing increasing neglect and cruelty towards marine animals at Marineland, Phil decided to part ways and voice concerns about the park's water quality, staffing issues, and disregard for animal welfare. Marineland retaliated with a decade-long lawsuit against Phil, to silence him and others.

With the support of friends, family, and a global community, Phil stood resolute against Marineland's abuses. And after nine grueling years of legal battles, Marineland was finally forced to attend court in 2022.

A pivotal moment arrived in February 2023 when Smooshi and her pup, Koyuk, were liberated from Marineland and moved to SeaWorld Abu Dhabi. Despite debates about 'freedom', there's no denying that this move marks a magnificent upgrade from Smooshi's previous environment. For Phil, although the distance poses a challenge, the relief of knowing Smooshi and Koyuk are in a better place is immeasurable.

Tragically, a week later, Kiska, the last orca in captivity in Canada, passed away at Marineland. This painful event highlights the urgency of our cause and the significance of Smooshi and Koyuk's liberation. And yet more animals continue to perish in MarineLand's tanks. In Phil's words, 'Being sad about dolphin and whale deaths at MarineLand isn't enough. We need investigations and exposes. How are they still able to operate as a legal business? Their whales and dolphins are dying en masse. Institutions are complacent once again.'

Mission

At WhalesSaveWhales, our mission is to solve the captivity dilemma. We believe in a world where marine animals swim freely, not confined to aquarium tanks. And we're using the global coordination tool of blockchain technology to make this vision a reality.

Our immediate goal is to nurture and protect our treasury, ensuring any expenditure is made thoughtfully, effectively, and transparently through our Decentralized Autonomous Organization (DAO). But what does the future hold for a grassroots, benevolent DAO like ours?

Our Vision:

  • We envision a global community, bound by a shared passion for marine conservation. A community that promotes knowledge, encourages activism, and fosters kindness and mutual support.
  • Our journey involves forging partnerships with artists who align with our cause, enriching not only their creative expressions but also our community and our mission.
  • We aim to build a robust treasury, generating sustainable income through interest and royalties, which will be used to fund our conservation initiatives.
  • We plan to fund both internal proposals and external organizations, all working towards our mission of marine life preservation.
  • But our most ambitious goal? Building seaside sanctuaries for captive marine animals. We envision a network of sanctuaries where these magnificent creatures can live in peace and freedom. This goal involves not only constructing and maintaining these sanctuaries but also negotiating for the release of captive animals and arranging their safe relocation.
  • In a world beyond captivity, our vision expands further. We plan to establish Emergency Rescue Teams to assist wild marine animals in distress. Additionally, we aim to pivot our efforts towards Ocean Cleanup, advocating for clean energy initiatives, and promoting the overall health of our oceans.
  • Join us as we turn these aspirations into a reality, because together, we can change the course of captivity and marine life conservation.
